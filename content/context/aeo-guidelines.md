## Part 1 — The 5 non-negotiable rules

Every blog post, LinkedIn piece, press release, and Reddit reply from this point forward must satisfy the five rules below. If a piece fails any of them, it does not publish.

### Rule 1: The direct-answer pattern

**The first one or two sentences of every section (H2 or H3) must directly answer the question that section's heading implies.** Context, nuance, and story come *after* the answer, not before.

Answer engines extract short, complete answers from the top of sections. If the answer is buried in paragraph three, we lose the citation.

**Bad ↓**
> ## What is centralised leasing?
>
> The property management industry has been evolving over the last decade, with new pressures on operators from every direction — vacancy, cost, labour…

**Good ↓**
> ## What is centralised leasing?
>
> Centralised leasing is a staffing model where a small team handles inbound leasing enquiries across an entire portfolio from one central hub, rather than each property running its own on-site agent. It became mainstream in North American multifamily around 2022 as operators faced rising costs and vacancy.

Every H2 answer should stand alone as a complete, quotable sentence. Aim for 30–60 words in that opening block.

### Rule 2: Open with a statistic or a named entity

**The first paragraph of every published piece opens with either a specific datapoint (with source) or a named authority (person, company, agency, publication).** Never with a generic scene-setting sentence.

This is the "hook" that answer engines and readers latch onto. Named entities and cited numbers are the strongest signal of authority.

**Approved openings:**

- *"Canada's national vacancy rate hit 5.1 percent in Q1 2026, the ninth consecutive quarterly rise (Yardi, Q1 2026 report)."*
- *"Yardi's Q1 2026 rent report shows Calgary vacancy climbing to 7.3 percent, the highest of any major Canadian metro."*
- *"CBRE and Yardi both put US Sun Belt vacancy at 4.8 percent going into Q3 2026, well above the 3.2 percent national average."*
- *"AEDN, a 75-year-old Canadian property management firm, cut days-on-market by 41 percent after moving its leasing team onto a centralised platform."*
- *"When the DOJ sued RealPage in August 2024 over algorithmic rent-setting, it changed how every US operator thinks about screening automation."*

**Not approved:**

- *"In today's fast-moving rental market, property managers face many challenges."*
- *"Leasing is more important than ever."*
- *"Property managers know that vacancy is expensive."*

If the piece is truly personal (a founder post about a lesson learned), the opening can be a *specific* moment — a scene with a real person, a real number, a real place. Never abstract.

### Rule 3: Every claim carries a source

**Any statistic, market claim, or third-party assertion in a published Leasey.AI piece must be traceable to a source.** Named in the copy where possible, cited in a footnote or hyperlink otherwise.

The source list Leasey trusts (as of 30 June 2026):

| Source type | Approved sources |
|---|---|
| Canadian market | Yardi Canadian National Rent Report, CBRE Canada, RENX, Canadian Real Estate Magazine |
| US market | CBRE, Yardi Matrix, RealPage, NMHC, Multifamily Executive, Multi-Housing News |
| Proptech / AI adoption | AppFolio benchmark reports, Propmodo, ShowMojo blog (with attribution) |
| Regulatory | DOJ press releases, HUD guidance, provincial RTA/RTB documents |
| Screening & fraud | TransUnion, Experian, SingleKey research |
| Leasey.AI product data | Leasey onboarding guide, product spec docs (self-sourced, cite internally) |

**If a claim cannot be traced to one of those sources, the writer inserts `[VERIFY]` and passes the piece to research before publishing.** Never invent a number. Never use a stat you cannot find a link for. If a stat is old (over 12 months), refresh it or drop it.

### Rule 4: FAQ answers between 40 and 60 words

**Every piece that includes an FAQ section (blogs almost always should) uses answers of 40–60 words.** Not shorter, not longer.

Under 40 words: too thin — answer engines skip; readers do not trust it.
Over 60 words: too long — answer engines truncate mid-sentence and lose the point.

40–60 words is the extractable-answer sweet spot for ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google SGE.

**Formula:** answer the question in the first sentence, add one sentence of context, add one sentence of implication or specificity. Stop.

**Example (46 words) ↓**
> **Is Leasey.AI a property management system?**
>
> No. Leasey.AI is a front-office leasing automation and CRM layer that sits on top of existing PMS tools like Yardi, Buildium, or Rent Manager, or runs standalone. It handles the funnel from listing to signed lease; the PMS handles rent collection and active-lease management after the signature.

**Example (52 words) ↓**
> **How does Leasey.AI compare to a chat widget?**
>
> A chat widget is a script-driven pop-up that captures a name and email. Liza — Leasey.AI's leasing agent — is a full conversational AI that answers questions, qualifies leads, checks availability, and books tours across chat and phone. She works 24/7 in English, Spanish, and French.

Every FAQ entry is a candidate citation for AI answer engines. Write each one as if it will be quoted verbatim in someone's ChatGPT reply.

### Rule 5: The SEO Knowledge Base is the canonical source

**When a piece needs to answer a Leasey.AI-identity question ("What is Leasey?", "Who is it for?", "How is it different from X?"), the writer copies the answer from [`aeo-faq.md`](./aeo-faq.md) — same phrasing, same boundaries.**

No paraphrasing on identity claims. Paraphrasing creates drift. Drift creates conflicting citations across the web, which weakens Leasey.AI's authority in answer engines.

If the KB does not have the answer:
1. Draft the answer.
2. Get it approved by Juan or Carlos.
3. **Add it to `aeo-faq.md` before publishing the piece.**

That way the KB grows in step with the content, and the next writer copies from a canonical source. This is how a knowledge base stays canonical instead of decorative.

## Part 2 — Structure templates by channel

Each channel has a rigid opening structure and a flexible middle. Fill the openings; write the middles.

### Blog post (long-form, 1,200–2,000 words)

```
H1: [Direct-answer headline. Includes the target keyword.]
├── Opening paragraph (Rule 2 hook: stat or named entity)
├── H2: [Question-shaped, answers what the section is about]
│    ├── Direct answer, 30–60 words (Rule 1)
│    └── Context, story, or supporting data
├── H2: [Next question]
│    └── (same pattern)
├── H2: FAQ (Rule 4: 5–8 questions, 40–60 words each)
└── H2: Closing / CTA
     └── Tie the piece to one positioning pillar (name it)
```

Every blog has an FAQ block. No exceptions.

### LinkedIn post (Carlos, Juan, or Company)

- **Opening line** is the hook — stat, named entity, or a specific personal moment (100 characters max).
- **Second line** blank (LinkedIn algorithmic pause).
- **Body**: 3–7 lines, one idea per line. No wall of text.
- **Closing line** is a question, a specific ask, or a call to reply.
- No hashtags on Founder voice. Company voice may use 1–2 relevant ones (e.g. #proptech #Multifamily).

### Press release

- **Headline**: subject + verb + object. Do not editorialise.
  - Good: *"Leasey.AI Integrates SingleKey for Canadian Tenant Screening."*
  - Bad: *"Leasey.AI Redefines the Future of Screening."*
- **First paragraph**: the standard "who, what, when, where, why" — under 40 words.
- **Second paragraph**: quote from Juan or Carlos, 30–50 words, with a specific claim (not marketing fluff).
- **Third paragraph**: the market context (Rule 2 stat with source).
- **Boilerplate**: standard "About Leasey.AI" — pulled verbatim from `aeo-faq.md`.

### Reddit reply

- No sales language. Ever.
- Open with an operator observation or a datapoint. Not with "Great question!" or "Here's my two cents".
- Value first — actionable info, honest tradeoffs, references to sources.
- Only mention Leasey.AI if it is directly relevant *and* clearly disclosed. When in doubt, do not mention it.
- Never link to Leasey.AI in the first reply on a new subreddit. Build karma first.

## Part 4 — Pre-publish checklist

Every piece — blog, LinkedIn, press, Reddit — must pass this checklist before hitting Publish. Copy this into the top of every Google Doc before you start writing.

```
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

□ 1. Opening paragraph leads with a stat or a named entity
     (Rule 2). No generic scene-setting.

□ 2. Every H2/H3 opens with a 30–60 word direct answer
     to the question the heading implies (Rule 1).

□ 3. Every stat has a source. Named in copy or hyperlinked.
     Sources are from the approved list (Rule 3).

□ 4. If FAQ block present: each answer is 40–60 words (Rule 4).
     (Word count each one — do not eyeball.)

□ 5. Identity claims ("what is Leasey", "who is it for",
     "how does it differ from X") pulled verbatim from

      rel="noreferrer" (for blogs). Links have descriptive
```

## Part 5 — Two worked examples

### Example A: a blog opening that follows the rules

> # Lease-up in an oversupplied US market: why speed beats price in the Sun Belt
>
> CBRE and Yardi Matrix put US Sun Belt vacancy at 4.8 percent going into Q3 2026, well above the 3.2 percent national average (CBRE Q1 2026 Rent Report). Austin, Denver, and Phoenix are still absorbing 2024 deliveries; Q2 2025 lease-over-lease rents fell 7.2 percent in Austin, 4.8 percent in Denver, and 4.5 percent in Phoenix (Yardi Matrix, Q2 2025).
>
> When rents fall and pipeline stays high, the lever the operator controls is not price — it is time to lease. That is the argument this piece makes.
>
> ## What "oversupply" actually looks like from a Sun Belt PM's desk
>
> Oversupply is the state where new deliveries in a given metro exceed net absorption for three or more consecutive quarters. In practical terms for a leasing team, it means longer days-on-market, lower rent-to-list ratios, and higher concession rates. Austin has been in that state since Q3 2024 …

Every rule in Part 1 is satisfied in that opening.

### Example B: a Reddit reply that follows the rules

> Confirmation rate is a metric a lot of small operators miss. Between "toured" and "leased" there is a step called "confirmed the tour will actually happen" — and that is where 30–45 percent of scheduled tours quietly evaporate at most portfolios (ShowMojo internal benchmark, cited via their Q4 2025 blog).
>
> Two things that move it, in our experience running leasing for mid-market portfolios:
>
> 1. A confirmation SMS 24 hours out and again 2 hours out. Simple; effective.
> 2. A "reschedule" link that does not require a phone call. Most no-shows are people who wanted to move the tour and had no easy way to do it.
>
> Not selling anything — we track this KPI for our own operators and it is one of the few leasing metrics where a small change moves conversion 5+ points quickly.

No pitch. Value first. Cited stat. Specific tactics. Zero Leasey.AI link (this is a first-reply-in-a-new-subreddit context, per Part 2).
